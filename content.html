Topography as a driving force

Topography controls water flow, sediment transport, inundation, landslides, and determines solar irradiation
As Doreen last time was talking about using computational models for simulating larval dispersal, in our group we work a lot with models simulating hydrological models, soil erosion, but also solar radiation. What these models have in common is that topography is the driving input to all these processes.

Terrain modification

GUI     vs.     TUI

To explore different scenarios with these models, it is quite difficult to modify topography in the form of digital elevation models on the computer, even using specialized software with nice graphical user interface. It is unintuitive and requires certain level of expertise. On the other hand, shaping things by hand is very natural and since it is effortless, it allows people to be more creative. We took advantage of this and developed a tangible user interface for the computational models, we call Tangible Landscape.

I will first explain the setup of the system. On the table we have a physical terrain model which is scanned by the scanner, in this case we use Microst Kinect for Windows, the gaming device. Above, there is projector, and then we computer where we run the geospatial analyses. We use GRASS GIS, an open source Geographic information system, where a wide range of analyses are implemented, from basic terrain analyses tools to more complicated hydrological models, erosion/deposition models. So how does this work?
We can change the surface of the physical model and our changes are then scanned and processed in the GIS and the results are projected back on the physical model so you can quickly see what are the impacts of your changes. The scan of the physical model is georeferenced and scaled to the real-world dimensions which ensures the results from the simulations are realistic.

We created the surface from sand so that we can modify it. Notice the road across the stream. So I scanned the model and ran the model on the scanned surface.
We can see it pretty quickly creates a pool just below the dam and it is slowed down by the elevated road.
Since the road is relatively new, built in 2003, we might ask, how is the waterflow affected by this road, what happens if it isn't there? So I just removed the road from the model.
Even though we removed the road and the water can spread more, it still spreads also uphill where the other stream is.
In the last scenario, I made the culvert very narrow to explore what happens if the culvert gets clogged. Since the water can't flow out, we get a large pool, eventually overflowing the road.

Tangible exploration of subsurface data

In the second case study, we will explote subsurface, specifically soil moisture data in the form of 3D raster or voxel model if you like. We can look under the ground and explore the place as if we were at an excavation site by digging into the sand with our hands.

Then we compute the cross-section of the scanned surface with the 3D raster representing soil properties and project it back on the surface.
